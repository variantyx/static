How to Report Bugs Effectively

Key insights from Simon Tatham's guide for clear and helpful bug reporting.

The goal of a bug report is simple: enable the programmer to see the program failing.

The Core Principle: Reproducibility

Your primary goal is to help the programmer reproduce the bug themselves. If they can make it fail, they can fix it.

  • Best case: Show them the bug in person. Let them see your screen and your actions.
  • Next best: Give them precise, step-by-step instructions to recreate the failure on their own machine.

Describe What Went Wrong

"It doesn't work" is not enough information. Be a detective and provide the clues.

What You Saw

Describe the actual outcome. "The program crashed," or "The text turned red."

What You Expected

Explain why the outcome was wrong. "I expected it to save the file," or "The text should have remained black."

Capture the Error Message

Error messages are the computer's attempt to explain what's wrong. They are vital clues, even if they look like nonsense to you.

Always provide the exact, complete text of any error message. If it includes numbers, include themâ€”they are often the most important part!

A vague report like "it gave an error" forces the programmer to guess. A verbatim message like "Exception 0xC0000005 at address 0x7C812345" gives them a specific place to start looking.

When Things Go Wrong: Freeze!

Your immediate reaction to a bug can either help or harm the investigation.

Don't be a Mongoose

Frantically clicking, trying random things, or reinstalling software can destroy valuable clues or make the problem worse.

Be an Antelope

When something unexpected happens, stop. Observe the screen. Note down everything unusual before you cautiously proceed.

Report Symptoms, Not Guesses

Just like with a doctor, describe the symptoms, not your self-diagnosed illness. Your theory might be helpful, but it's not a substitute for the facts.

  • Bad: "The network buffer is overflowing." (This is a guess).
  • Good: "When I upload a file larger than 10MB, the connection drops after 30 seconds." (These are the facts/symptoms).

Always separate facts ("I saw...") from speculation ("I think...").

Handling Tricky Bugs

Some bugs don't happen every time or only happen on your machine. In these cases, environment details are critical.

Look for patterns. Does it only happen with large files? When another program is running? After the computer has been on for a while?

Be ready to provide:

  • Program version number
  • Operating System version (e.g., Windows 11, macOS Sonoma)
  • Versions of any related software
  • Details about your hardware if it seems relevant

Clarity is Kindness

An unclear report can be worse than no report at all. Make it easy for the programmer to understand you.

  • 1

    Be Specific

    Instead of "I selected Load," say "I pressed Alt-L to open the Load menu."

  • 2

    Be Verbose

    More information is better than less. Don't make the programmer ask for every detail one sentence at a time.

  • 3

    Avoid Ambiguous Pronouns

    Don't say "I tried to close it and it crashed." Say "I tried to close the warning window, and the main application crashed."

Summary: The Perfect Bug Report

To make programmers happy and get bugs fixed faster, remember to:

  • Be precise and provide step-by-step instructions.
  • Describe what you saw and what you expected to see.
  • Include the exact text of any error messages.
  • Report facts and symptoms, not just guesses.
  • Be ready to provide version numbers and system details.
  • Write clearly and review your report before sending.
